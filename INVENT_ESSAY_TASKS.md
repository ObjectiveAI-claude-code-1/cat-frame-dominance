Task 1 – Evaluate Proportional Presence: Assess how much of the image the cat physically occupies relative to the total frame. Determine whether the cat is generously scaled — large enough to feel like the undeniable reason the photo was taken — while also appearing whole and complete within the image boundaries. A cat that fills a substantial portion of the frame should score highly, but a cat that overflows the edges and feels clipped or fragmented should be penalized, as should a cat that is so small it reads as a minor element in a larger scene. The ideal score reflects a cat that is both big enough to command attention and complete enough to be fully appreciated, striking the balance between generous scale and comfortable containment.

Task 2 – Evaluate Positional Authority: Assess where the cat is located within the frame and whether that placement communicates visual importance. Determine how close the cat is to the center of the image or to the strong focal intersections along the rule-of-thirds grid lines. A cat positioned centrally or along these natural points of visual gravity should score highly, as its placement feels deliberate and commanding. A cat pushed to the far edges, buried in a corner, or crowded against the margins of the frame should score low, as its position suggests it was caught incidentally rather than framed intentionally. This task measures whether the cat's placement within the geometry of the image makes the viewer perceive it as the subject rather than a peripheral detail.

Task 3 – Evaluate Visual Isolation: Assess how cleanly the cat stands apart from its surrounding environment and how free the rest of the frame is from competing visual elements. Determine whether the background is simple, uncluttered, or softly blurred in a way that acts as a stage for the cat, or whether it is busy, chaotic, and full of objects that pull the viewer's eye away from the cat. A cat set against a cooperative, quiet background should score highly because there is nothing else to challenge it for attention. A cat surrounded by visual clutter — furniture, patterns, other objects, strong textures — should score low because the environment undermines the cat's role as the clear subject. This task measures the signal-to-noise ratio of the image, where the cat is the signal and everything else is noise.
