Task 1 — Measure Proportional Presence: Determine how much of the image the cat physically occupies relative to the full frame. The function must assess the spatial ratio between the cat and the total image area, judging whether the cat is large enough to be unmistakably the main subject. This is not simply a matter of raw size — the cat should feel substantially and completely there. A cat that fills the frame but is severely cropped, showing only a fragment of itself, is less present than a cat whose whole form is visible and prominent. The function should reward images where the cat's body, face, and posture are legible and take up meaningful room, and penalize images where the cat is a small, distant, or hard-to-identify shape that requires effort to find. Proportional presence is the foundation upon which all other dominance depends; without sufficient size, a cat cannot anchor an image regardless of any other factor.

Task 2 — Evaluate Positional Authority: Assess where the cat is placed within the geometry of the frame and whether that placement feels prominent and commanding. The function must judge the cat's position relative to the compositional structure of the image — whether it sits in or near the center, along natural focal lines, or in areas where the human eye is naturally drawn. A cat does not need to be perfectly centered to score well; what matters is that its placement feels deliberate and dominant, pulling the viewer's attention toward it rather than away. The function should reward cats whose position creates compositional gravity — a sense that the image was framed around them — and penalize cats that are pushed to the periphery, tucked into corners, or placed in ways that feel accidental, as though the cat wandered into someone else's photograph. Positional authority captures the principle that where a subject sits in the frame matters just as much as how large it is.

Task 3 — Assess Environmental Clarity: Evaluate the degree to which the space surrounding the cat defers to it rather than competing with it for the viewer's attention. The function must examine the background and context of the image, judging whether the environment is clean, calm, and subordinate to the cat, or noisy, cluttered, and distracting. A simple background — a plain wall, a soft blanket, a blurred backdrop — amplifies the cat's dominance by giving the viewer's eye nowhere else to go. A busy environment filled with objects, text, bright colors, or visual clutter fragments focus and dilutes the cat's authority, even if the cat itself is large and well-placed. This task must also account for competing subjects: other animals, people, or prominent visual elements that divide attention and prevent the cat from being the sole commanding presence. The function should reward images where everything that is not the cat quietly steps aside, and penalize images where the cat must fight for attention against its surroundings.
