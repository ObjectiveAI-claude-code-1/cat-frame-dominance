# Essay Tasks

## Task 1: Evaluate Proportional Presence

Determine how much of the image's total area the cat occupies, and whether that occupation feels substantial and complete. The function must assess the fraction of the frame that belongs to the cat — a cat filling most of the image scores highly, while a cat reduced to a small speck in a vast scene scores poorly. However, this evaluation must account for completeness: a cat whose body is generously and wholly contained within the frame is more dominant than one that has been so aggressively cropped that ears, paws, or tail are cut off, leaving the viewer with the sense of a fragmented subject rather than a commanding one. The ideal is a cat that fills the frame with its full form — or at least its full face and upper body — feeling significant in size without feeling incomplete. This task produces a score reflecting the cat's share of visual real estate, tempered by how whole and intact the cat appears within the image boundaries.

## Task 2: Evaluate Positional Authority

Determine where the cat is located within the frame and whether that placement conveys visual importance and compositional intention. The function must assess whether the cat occupies a position of natural power — such as the center of the image or along the intersecting lines of thirds that photographers use to compose compelling subjects — versus being pushed to the extreme edges or corners where it feels marginalized and incidental. A cat placed centrally in a symmetrical composition, or positioned along a strong focal line with balanced space around it, radiates deliberate dominance. A cat awkwardly near the border of the frame, as if barely caught before it walked out of the shot, feels accidental and unimportant. This task produces a score reflecting whether the cat's position within the image communicates that this is where the subject belongs — placed with purpose rather than captured by chance.

## Task 3: Evaluate Subject Isolation

Determine how cleanly the cat separates from its surroundings and how free the image is from competing visual elements that pull the viewer's attention away. The function must assess whether the background and environment around the cat are quiet and uncluttered — allowing the cat to stand forward as the singular, unambiguous focus — or whether the scene is busy with patterned furniture, scattered objects, bright competing colors, and other visual noise that forces the cat to fight for attention. A cat photographed against a smooth, simple background achieves high isolation: there is nothing else for the eye to latch onto. A cat surrounded by a chaotic environment, even if large and centrally placed, has its dominance diluted because the viewer's eye wanders to competing elements. This task produces a score reflecting whether the image's visual environment surrenders attention to the cat or forces it to compete — capturing the difference between a picture that contains a cat and a picture that is about a cat.
