1. Evaluate the cat's proportional presence within the frame. Determine how much of the image the cat physically occupies relative to the total space available. A cat whose body fills a generous portion of the photograph carries inherent visual weight — it is large enough to command attention and difficult to overlook. However, proportional presence is not simply about maximizing size. A cat that is cropped so aggressively that significant parts of its body are clipped out of frame can feel incomplete, as though the viewer is seeing a fragment rather than a whole subject. The ideal proportional presence is a cat that appears both large and complete — generously sized within the boundaries of the image while still feeling fully contained. A cat that is small or tiny within a vast scene scores low, a cat that fills the frame with a sense of wholeness scores high, and a cat that is technically large but visibly incomplete falls somewhere in between.

2. Evaluate the cat's positional authority within the frame. Determine where the cat is situated relative to the natural focal points of the image — the center, the rule-of-thirds intersections, and the overall visual stage of the composition. A cat positioned at or near these points of natural visual gravity benefits from the geometry of human attention; the eye is drawn there instinctively. A cat placed prominently in the heart of the image radiates intentionality and ownership of the scene. Conversely, a cat pushed to the far edges or corners of the frame — no matter how large — can feel like it is on the verge of leaving the picture rather than commanding it. A cat squeezed into the top margin or sinking into the bottom feels secondary to the space around it. This task assesses whether the cat is positioned where the eye expects to find the most important thing in the photograph, occupying the compositional ground that signals it is the subject rather than a bystander.

3. Evaluate the visual clarity of the cat as the unambiguous subject of the image. Determine how cleanly the cat separates from its surroundings and whether the environment cooperates in directing attention toward the cat or competes against it. A cat photographed against a simple, uncluttered background — a plain wall, an open sky, a clean surface — benefits from having nothing else in the frame that pulls the viewer's eye away. A cat surrounded by clutter, busy patterns, competing objects, or other visually demanding elements loses authority because the viewer's attention is fragmented across multiple points of interest. Visual clarity also encompasses how distinguishable the cat is from its background; a cat that blends into its surroundings through similar color, low contrast, heavy shadow, or camouflage against a busy pattern is harder to perceive as a distinct subject, even if it is large and well-positioned. This task measures whether the cat stands apart from everything else in the image with enough separation, contrast, and freedom from competition to be immediately and unmistakably recognized as the thing the photograph is about.
