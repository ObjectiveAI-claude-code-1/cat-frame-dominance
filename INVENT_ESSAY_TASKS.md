1. Evaluate the cat's proportional occupation of the frame. Determine how much of the image the cat physically fills, considering not just raw spatial coverage but whether the cat feels wholly and comfortably present within the frame. A cat that occupies a generous portion of the image commands more visual weight, but a close-up that clips the cat's body and leaves the subject feeling incomplete should not be rewarded the same as one where the cat is substantially and deliberately contained. Score higher when the cat's scale relative to the frame feels generous and authoritative, and lower when the cat is small, distant, or takes up only a tiny fraction of the available space.

2. Evaluate the cat's positional prominence within the frame. Determine where the cat is placed relative to the center of the image and the natural focal lines where the human eye tends to rest, such as the rule-of-thirds intersections. A cat positioned in these zones of visual gravity benefits from the way human perception works — the eye arrives at it immediately and without effort. Score higher when the cat is centrally placed or sits along prominent compositional lines, and lower when the cat is pushed to the edges, tucked into corners, or positioned in areas of the frame where the viewer must search to find it.

3. Evaluate the scene clarity surrounding the cat. Determine the degree to which the background and surrounding environment yield to the cat, allowing it to stand as the unambiguous subject. Consider whether the space around the cat is clean and simple — letting the cat visually speak without competition — or whether it is cluttered, chaotic, and filled with competing visual elements that pull the viewer's attention away. Also consider whether objects partially obstruct the cat, whether patterns or colors camouflage it, or whether the cat blends into its surroundings in a way that makes it visually unclear. Score higher when the scene cooperates with the cat's dominance, and lower when visual noise, clutter, or obstructions undermine the cat's authority as the clear subject.
