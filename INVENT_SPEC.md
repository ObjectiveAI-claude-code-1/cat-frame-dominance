Evaluate the frame dominance of a single cat picture. Determine how much the cat commands the physical space of the image. Assess whether the cat is the undeniable focal point — the thing the eye goes to first and stays on longest. Consider how much of the frame the cat occupies, whether it is centrally or prominently positioned, and whether the surrounding environment is clean enough to let the cat stand as the clear subject. A cat that fills the frame with visual authority should score high (closer to 1). A cat that is small, obscured, off to the side, competing with clutter, or barely distinguishable from its surroundings should score low (closer to 0). Frame dominance is not simply about size — a cat photographed from across a room may still dominate the frame if it is centrally positioned against an uncluttered background. Conversely, a close-up that clips half the cat out of frame may feel less dominant because the subject feels incomplete. This evaluation answers the foundational question: is this picture actually about the cat? When building sub-functions, focus on evaluating the cat's proportional size within the frame, its positioning relative to the center and focal lines, the degree of background clutter or competing visual elements, and the overall sense that the cat is the clear, unambiguous subject of the image.
